§1

Meeting at a vertex

Set k equilateral triangles around a single point. Their angles sum to k · 60°. A convex vertex needs this strictly under a full turn; flatness is the boundary case:

k · 60° ≤ 360°  ⟹  k ∈ {3, 4, 5, 6}.

Four values, no more. Below k = 3 nothing closes; above k = 6 the angle sum exceeds a turn and the surface goes hyperbolic. The four admissible counts are the entire structure.

§3

The residue, derived

At each vertex an angle of a full turn goes unfilled. That shortfall is the angular deficit — the curvature concentrated at the vertex, and the quantity this plate calls the holographic residue:

δ(k) = 360° − k · 60° = (6 − k) · 60°.

The flat tiling (k = 6) has δ = 0: a boundary with no curvature — the holographic screen, on which information can be laid flat. Every other closure carries residue, and the residue is the break from flatness. By Gauss–Bonnet (Descartes' theorem), the residue summed over all vertices is conserved — the same 720° = 4π for every closed solid:

The 4-fold and 6-fold traces are whole numbers; their figures (the octahedron's square sections, the hexagonal plane) sit on the flat screen without residue of irrationality. The 5-fold trace is φ — irrational — so no flat lattice can hold it. It is forced onto a curved surface: the icosahedron. This is the validation of the broken residue. The break is not posited; it is the trace failing to be an integer.
